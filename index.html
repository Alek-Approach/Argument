🔍 Notice It≈ 10 min

How does it sound?

Listen again with the transcript open. Then discuss with a partner and write short answers.

  1. Read the argument aloud with the pauses. Where does the speaker slow down? Where do they speed up? What does this pacing do for the argument's clarity?
  2. The speaker says: "And THIS is the argument I find most COMPELLING." Both words are stressed. What does stressing THIS tell the listener? What would change if those words were not stressed?
  3. The final four thought groups: "But I am claiming / that it is a question / worth asking. / Seriously." — each one lands separately. Why break one sentence into four groups? What effect does "Seriously." — a single-word thought group — create?
🔊 Pronunciation Focus≈ 10 min

Thought groups structure the logic

In an argument, pauses do more than help understanding — they are the structure. A pause before "but" signals a change of direction. A pause after a key word lets it register. Short, separate thought groups at the end create finality and authority.

Marked exampleWhat to notice
First — / local elections determine / who controls schools. The pause after "First" signals a list is coming; the pauses inside show the logic building step by step.
I'm not claiming this is simple. / It isn't. Short and final — the brevity signals confidence and authority.
Worth asking. / Seriously. Two tiny groups — the listener cannot rush past "Seriously."

Drill — say these aloud

Mark the stressed syllable in CAPITALS, then practice in pairs. Exaggerate the pauses first, then make them natural.

First — / Second — / and this is key — / Here's my reasoning. / I'm not claiming ___. / I am claiming ___. / Worth discussing. / Seriously.
🗣 Speaking Task A≈ 20 min

2-minute argument

Choose one topic. Take 5 minutes to prepare notes (key words only — no full sentences). Then deliver a 2-minute argument, using thought-group pauses deliberately.

  • Should employers be required to accommodate religious practices at work?
  • Should undocumented immigrants be eligible for in-state college tuition?
  • Is healthcare a right or a privilege?

Use this structure

Here's my reasoning. / First — / … Second — / … Third — and most importantly — / …
Close: I'm not claiming this is simple. / But I am claiming / it is worth taking seriously.

🗣 Speaking Task B≈ 20 min

An argument that matters to you

Prepare a 2-minute argument about something you genuinely believe — about immigration, education, healthcare, or your own community.

  1. What is your position? State it in one clear sentence.
  2. Give three reasons. Use "First," "Second," "Third — and most importantly." Write them in thought-group form — use / to mark your pauses.
  3. Acknowledge the counterargument honestly. What would someone who disagrees say?
  4. Close with a final thought that lands. Keep it short — one or two small thought groups.
2:00 Speaking timer — 2 minutes
